Framework and benchmark

AgriManager

A framework and benchmark for LLM-RL generalization in agricultural management.

AgriManager studies whether prompt-conditioned policies can generalize across changing weather, crops, prices, observations, actions, objectives, and simulator dynamics.

Motivation

Generalization in agriculture is not just about new weather.

Agricultural policies face two forms of heterogeneity. The first is distributional: weather, crops, prices, years, and locations change while the interface stays fixed. The second is structural: farms and simulators may expose different observations, action menus, and objectives.

Fixed-interface neural policies become incompatible when the observation vector or action head changes. AgriManager instead makes the policy-facing schema explicit in text, so a prompt-conditioned policy can read the task interface at the start of each episode.

Axis 1

Distributional shift

The schema stays fixed, but the context changes: weather regimes, crop traits, prices, years, or locations.

Axis 2

Schema shift

The policy-facing interface changes: observed variables, available actions, or reward objectives differ across settings.

Policy class

Prompt-conditioned control

The LLM policy receives the current schema as text and emits a structured action in the same episode-specific interface.

Framework

One prompt interface over multiple crop simulators.

01 Simulator adapters

WOFOST-Gym, CycleGym, and DSSAT-Gym are connected through backend-specific adapters.

02 Schema-aware prompts

Observation fields, action menus, and objectives are declared in every episode prompt.

03 LLM-RL rollouts

Prompt-conditioned policies are trained with GRPO via VeRL and compared against NN baselines where applicable.

Benchmark

A three-tier generalization ladder.

The benchmark separates context shift from schema shift, then recombines them across simulator backends.

T1

Distributional shift

Can a policy handle new contexts under the same observation, action, and reward schema?

  • Weather-regime shift
  • Cross-crop trait shift
  • Price-regime shift
T2

Single-axis schema shift

What happens when one part of the policy-facing interface changes?

  • Observation-schema shift
  • Action-menu shift
  • Reward-formulation shift
T3

Compositional shift

Can one policy compose simulator and schema coverage into held-out combinations?

  • Cross-simulator transfer
  • Unified policy evaluation

Findings

What the benchmark shows.

01

LLM-RL is competitive under fixed schemas.

Within fixed schemas, prompt-conditioned LLM-RL matches or exceeds fixed-interface NN-RL across weather, crop-trait, and price shifts.

02

Reasoning helps under cost-sensitive rewards.

Explicit reasoning is most useful when the reward requires balancing yield against resource cost and input penalties.

03

Schema axes fail differently.

Observation shift stresses information sufficiency, action-menu shift stresses joint action coverage, and reward shift stresses objective coverage.

04

Unified policies work with coverage.

A unified prompt-conditioned policy can match or exceed per-source specialists when simulator, action, and objective components receive marginal coverage during training.

Abstract

Formal summary.

Agricultural management is heterogeneous along two axes. The first is distributional shift within a fixed task interface: the same sensors, action menu, and objective, but with weather, planted crops, and prices varying across years and locations. The second is structural shift across interfaces: different farms expose different observed variables, available actions, and management objectives.

AgriManager formalizes structural variation as schema shift and unifies DSSAT, WOFOST, and Cycles under prompt-based schema specification. The benchmark introduces a three-tier ladder spanning within-schema distributional shift, single-axis schema shift, and compositional schema shift across simulators.

Limitations

Simulator benchmark, not farm advice.

AgriManager is a research benchmark for evaluating policy generalization in simulation. It is not a farm-deployment system or agronomic recommendation service. Results are bounded by simulator assumptions, scenario-generation choices, available crop/weather regimes, and the gap between simulated dynamics and real farm operations.
